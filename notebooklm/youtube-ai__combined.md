**Source:** [YouTube Help — Create content for Shorts using AI-generated features](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/15260303)

## Overview

YouTube offers **experimental AI-generated creation tools for Shorts** that allow creators to generate **unique images or videos** from prompts. These can be used as:

- **Green screen backgrounds**
- **Standalone video clips**
- **Photo-to-video Shorts**
- **“Make me move” animated clips**
- Other AI Playground assets such as **backgrounds, tracks, and effects**

These tools are intended to help creators bring imaginative ideas to life directly inside the YouTube app.

> “These content creation tools are experimental AI-generated features from YouTube that let you bring your imagination to life.”

---

## Policy, Safety, and Disclosure Guidance

### Community Guidelines Still Apply

All content created with YouTube’s AI tools must follow YouTube’s **Community Guidelines**.

> “All content on YouTube, including AI-generated creations from these tools, must follow our Community Guidelines.”

Creators remain responsible for their published content, including content created with AI.

### Built-In Safeguards

YouTube has added safeguards to its AI tools to help reduce inappropriate or policy-violating outputs.

The tools may block:

- Prompts that violate YouTube policies
- Prompts involving sensitive topics
- Attempts to generate certain harmful or misleading content

Example:

> “Our AI tools are designed to help creators avoid generating photorealistic images of identifiable people, which could be misused for creating deep fakes or other harmful content.”

### Review Before Publishing

YouTube notes that generative AI tools can make mistakes, so creators should carefully review outputs before uploading.

> “As with any generative AI tool, mistakes are possible and we may not always get it right.”

### AI Disclosure

Creators using YouTube’s own generative AI tools **do not need to manually disclose** altered or synthetic content.

> **Note:** Creators who make a post or YouTube Short using YouTube's generative artificial intelligence (AI) tools don't need to take extra steps to disclose the use of altered or synthetic content. The tool will automatically disclose the use of AI for creators.

# Create, Use & Manage Your Avatar on YouTube — Summary

**Source:** YouTube Help — “Create, use & manage your avatar on YouTube”  
**URL:** https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/16985237

---

## Overview

YouTube avatars let creators generate videos featuring a digital version of themselves that can look and sound like them. The feature builds on YouTube’s AI “ingredients-to-video” creation tools and is available through:

- The **YouTube mobile app**
- The **YouTube Create app**

Avatars are created using a secure **“live selfie”** capture process that records your face and voice. Once created, the avatar can be used to generate AI videos from text prompts.

---

## Key Excerpts

> “An avatar builds on existing ingredients-to-video features in YouTube’s creation tools, making it easier and more accurate to add yourself into your videos.”

> “Avatars create a digital version of yourself so you can generate videos that look and sound like you, safely and securely.”

> “Only you can use your avatar to create original videos, you can delete it at any time, and all output is labeled as AI.”

> **Important:** “The YouTube AI Features Privacy Notice applies to avatars on YouTube.”

> **Note:** “This feature will be rolling out gradually.”

> **Important:** “Avatars and videos are managed separately. Deleting your avatar will not automatically delete the videos you published or their remixes. Deleting a video with your avatar will not delete your avatar from your account.”

> **Note:** “If your avatar is not used to create new content for 3 years, it will be automatically deleted.”

---

To create a YouTube avatar, you must be:

- **18 years or older**
- **The channel owner** of an existing YouTube channel

To make sure your avatar recording is accepted, follow these guidelines:

- **Phone position:** Hold your phone at eye level and stay centered on screen.
- **Lighting:** Use balanced lighting that is not too dim or too bright.
- **Visibility:** Make sure your eyes, nose, and mouth are visible.
  - Glasses are allowed.
  - Do **not** wear sunglasses, masks, or hats.
- **Audio environment:** Record in a quiet place with no other voices in the background.
- **Background:** Make sure there are no people or images of faces behind you.
- **Account owner:** The account owner must complete the process.
  - Do **not** allow minors to record themselves.
- **Selfie direction:** Follow the prompted action, then return your face to the center.

# YouTube Help: Disclosing Use of GenAI Content

**Source:** YouTube Help — “Disclosing use of GenAI content”  
**URL:** https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/14328491

---

## Core Requirement

YouTube requires creators to disclose when they use AI to **meaningfully alter or generate realistic content**, especially when viewers could reasonably believe what they are seeing or hearing is real.

> “To help keep viewers informed about the content they're viewing, we require creators to disclose when they use AI to meaningfully alter or generate photorealistic content.”

Creators must disclose GenAI content that:

- **Makes a real person appear to say or do something they didn’t do.**
- **Alters footage of a real event or place.**
- **Generates a realistic scene that didn’t actually occur.**

This applies to content that is **fully or partially altered or created using AI tools**, including AI audio, video, image creation, or editing tools.

---

## What Counts as GenAI Content Requiring Disclosure?

Disclosure is required when content is:

- **AI-generated or meaningfully AI-altered**
- **Realistic or photorealistic**
- Capable of misleading viewers about what actually happened, what someone said, or what someone did

YouTube emphasizes that the examples provided are **not exhaustive**.

---

## Examples Creators Do **Not** Have to Disclose

Creators generally do **not** need to disclose:

- **Non-realistic AI content**
- **Minor edits** to realistic content
- Edits that are primarily aesthetic and do not mislead viewers about reality

> “Minor edits are ones that are primarily aesthetic, and don't alter the content in a way that could mislead the viewer about what actually happened.”

### Non-Realistic Content

Disclosure is not required for examples such as:

- Someone riding a unicorn through a fantastical world
- Green screen used to depict someone floating in space
- Using an AI-generated or altered animation of a missile in a fully animated video

### Minor Edits or AI Assistance

Disclosure is not required for:

- Applying beauty filters
- Color adjustment or lighting filters
- Special effects filters, such as background blur or vintage effects
- Production assistance, such as using generative AI tools to create or improve:
  - Video outlines
  - Scripts
  - Thumbnails
  - Titles
  - Infographics
- Caption creation
- Video sharpening, upscaling, or repair
- Voice or audio repair
- Idea generation
- Cloning one’s own voice to create voiceovers or dubs
- Gameplay footage from video games
- AI generating or extending a backdrop to simulate a moving car
- Using effects to enhance previously recorded audio

---

## Examples Creators **Need** to Disclose

Creators must disclose AI-generated or meaningfully AI-altered content when it appears realistic.

Examples requiring disclosure include:

- AI-generated music
- AI-generated extra footage of a real place, such as a video of a surfer in Maui for a promotional travel video
- AI-generated realistic videos of a match between two real professional tennis players
- Making it appear as if someone gave advice they did not actually give
- Showing a realistic depiction of a tornado or other weather event moving toward a real city when it did not actually happen
- Making it appear as if hospital workers turned away sick or wounded patients
- Depicting a public figure stealing something they did not steal
- Depicting a public figure admitting to stealing something when they did not make that admission
- Making it look like a real person has been arrested or imprisoned

---

## How to Disclose GenAI Content

Creators disclose AI-generated or meaningfully AI-altered realistic content during the upload process in YouTube Studio.

### Steps on Computer

1. Go to [YouTube Studio](https://studio.youtube.com/).
2. Follow the steps to upload content.
3. In the **Attributes** section, under **“AI use,”** select:
   - **Yes** if the content meets the disclosure requirements
   - **No** if it does not
4. Continue selecting other video details.

The AI disclosure setting is available in YouTube Studio on both **computer and mobile devices**.

---

## Labels for AI Content

If a creator selects **“Yes”** to indicate that content is realistic and generated or meaningfully altered with AI, YouTube will add a label to the video.

### Possible Label Placements

Labels may appear in different places depending on the type of content:

- **Video player label**  
  For photorealistic AI content, a label may appear directly in the video player.

- **Expanded description label**  
  For non-photorealistic or animated AI content, labels may appear in the expanded description field.

> **Note:** Disclosing AI content won’t limit a video’s audience or impact its eligibility to earn money.

# Understanding “How this content was made” Disclosures on YouTube — Summary

**Source:** YouTube Help — <https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/15447836>

## Core Purpose

YouTube may show information explaining **how a video was made**. These disclosures can appear in the **video player** or **video description**, especially in the expanded description section labeled **“How this content was made.”**

These notices help viewers understand:

- The **origin** of the content
- Whether the content was **meaningfully altered**
- Whether the content was **synthetically generated**, including with AI
- Whether disclosure information came from creators, YouTube tools, or secure metadata such as **Content Credentials / C2PA**

---

## Key Excerpts

> While watching YouTube, you might find information about how content was made. This information, found in the video player or description, can offer insight into the content's origin and whether it has been meaningfully altered.

> The "How this content was made" section in the expanded video description provides further details.

> ‘Altered or synthetic content’: This indicates that a video contains content that has been meaningfully altered or synthetically generated.

> YouTube will carry forward disclosures from tools and creators available with secure Content Credentials (C2PA) 2.1 or higher that indicate the entire video was made with AI.

> Content Credential (C2PA) disclosures often include an attribution to the signing authority, which indicates the source that validated the information within the secure Content Credentials.

---

## Disclosure Type: “Altered or Synthetic Content”

The disclosure **“Altered or synthetic content”** means a video includes content that has been:

- **Meaningfully altered**, or
- **Synthetically generated**, such as by AI

This disclosure may appear in the **“How this content was made”** section of the expanded video description.

---

## When Viewers Should Expect This Disclosure

YouTube says viewers should expect to see an **altered or synthetic content** disclosure when one of the following applies:

1. **The creator manually discloses it**
   - The creator indicates in the **YouTube Studio workflow** that the video contains altered or synthetic content.
   - If creators do not disclose such content, YouTube may sometimes apply a label itself.

   > When content is undisclosed, in some cases, YouTube may take action to reduce the risk of harm to viewers by proactively applying a label that creators will not have the option to remove.

2. **The creator uses YouTube’s generative AI tools**
   - Example given: **DreamScreen**

3. **The video contains valid Content Credentials data**
   - Specifically, data indicating that **the entire video is made with AI**
   - YouTube references **Content Credentials / C2PA** as the standard involved.

---

## Content Credentials / C2PA

YouTube may use **Content Credentials**, also known as **C2PA**, to carry forward disclosure information.

### Important Details

- YouTube supports disclosures from secure Content Credentials using **C2PA 2.1 or higher**.
- These disclosures may come from:
  - External tools
  - Creators
  - Signing authorities that validate metadata
- The disclosure may appear:
  - As a **label on the video player**
  - As language in the **detailed / expanded video description**

### AI-Specific Use

YouTube carries forward disclosures when secure Content Credentials indicate:

- The **entire video was made with AI**

---

## “Info from” Attributions

Some C2PA-based disclosures include an **“Info from”** attribution.

This attribution identifies the **signing authority** — the source that validated the information inside the secure Content Credentials.

> Content Credential (C2PA) disclosures often include an attribution to the signing authority, which indicates the source that validated the information within the secure Content Credentials.

When this applies, viewers will see an **“Info from”** attribution beneath the disclosure.

---

## Related Disclosure: “Captured with a Camera”

YouTube notes that Content Credentials may also be used for other types of content-origin disclosures, including:

- **“Captured with a camera”**

This is a separate disclosure type from **altered or synthetic content**.

---

## Practical Takeaways

- YouTube’s **“How this content was made”** section helps viewers evaluate whether a video was AI-generated, synthetic, or meaningfully altered.
- The **“Altered or synthetic content”** label can come from:
  - Creator self-disclosure
  - YouTube’s own AI tools
  - Secure Content Credentials metadata
  - YouTube-applied labels in some undisclosed cases
- Some labels may be **non-removable by creators** if YouTube applies them to reduce risk of harm.
- **C2PA 2.1+ Content Credentials** can allow disclosure information to persist across platforms and tools.
- **“Info from”** tells viewers who validated the disclosure information.

## Overview

YouTube’s **automatic dubbing** feature generates translated audio tracks for videos to make them more accessible globally.

> “Automatic dubbing generates translated audio tracks in different languages to make your videos more accessible to viewers around the world.”

Videos with automatic dubs are labeled as **“auto-dubbed”** in the video description.

Viewers can:
- Set preferred languages for **audio, titles, and descriptions**
- Switch between the **original audio** and **dubbed audio tracks** on individual videos

For eligible creators, dubs are generated automatically when a new video is uploaded. YouTube may also generate dubs for older, previously published videos over time.

---

## Key Behavior & Publication

- New uploads may receive automatic dubs.
- Dubs are published based on your **channel publication settings**.
- Creators may choose to:
  - Publish dubs automatically
  - Manually review dubs before publication
  - Review all dubs
  - Review only experimental-language dubs
- Dubs may be regenerated over time to improve quality.

---

## Quality, Limitations & Eligibility

Automatic dubbing is improving, but quality can vary by language and source audio.

> “We cannot yet successfully and accurately dub all videos, but we’re continually working on improvements.”

Automatic dubs may contain errors due to:
- Mispronunciations
- Accents or dialects
- Background noise
- Translation challenges with:
  - Proper nouns
  - Idioms
  - Jargon
- Speech recognition issues
- Difficulty matching dubbed voice to the original voice

Some languages support **expressive speech**, which:

> “replicates the original audio's pitch and intonation to produce more natural-sounding dubs.”

---

## Reasons a Video May Be Ineligible

A video may not qualify for automatic dubbing for these reasons:

- **Video Length:** The video exceeds **120 minutes**
- **Audio Content:**
  - No speech
  - Only music
  - Very little spoken content
  - Original audio is in an unsupported language
- **Source Language Detection:** YouTube cannot automatically detect the source language
- **Speech Pacing:** Speech is too fast, which would create an unlistenable sped-up dub
- **Content ID Claims:** The video contains copyrighted content

---

## Best Practices

To improve dub accuracy:

- Set the **original video language** correctly when uploading.
- Review dubs before publishing if quality control is important.
- Ask someone who speaks the target language to review dubs if needed.

Important limitation:

> “You cannot edit automatic dubs.”

Creators can only preview, review, publish, unpublish, or delete dubs.
